\begin{abstract}
Classification of cancer subtypes from high-dimensional genomic data presents a significant challenge due to the "curse of dimensionality," where gene expression features ($p=60{,}660$) vastly exceed available samples ($n \approx 600$). This study benchmarks the performance and computational trade-offs of filter, wrapper, and embedded feature selection methods using TCGA datasets (BRCA, COAD, and PRAD). We evaluate these techniques against a high-dimensional Logistic Regression baseline to assess impact on predictive accuracy, Macro F1 scores, and runtime efficiency. Our results demonstrate that while ANOVA and Mutual Information can reduce the feature space by over 90\% (to $p=2{,}000$ or $5{,}000$), they do not necessarily incur performance trade-offs; in several cohorts, filter methods outperformed the full-feature baseline, such as an increase in BRCA Macro F1 from 0.7268 to 0.7715. However, severe class imbalance in the PRAD dataset remained a challenge, with Macro F1 scores plateauing near 0.42 despite high raw accuracy. We conclude that for genomic data, strategic feature selection serves as a critical regularizing step that can mitigate noise and surpass the performance of high-dimensional models that utilize the full transcriptomic profile.
\end{abstract}















\section{Introduction}

The classification of cancer subtypes is a fundamental challenge in precision oncology. Accurate identification of these subtypes is critical for predicting disease progression and tailoring therapeutic interventions to individual patients. Traditionally, cancer diagnosis relied on histopathological examination; however, the advent of high-throughput sequencing has allowed researchers to utilize gene expression profiles to identify molecular signatures that differentiate subtypes with higher resolution. In this study, we utilize transcriptomic data from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), focusing on three cohorts with varying degrees of complexity:
\begin{itemize}
    \item TCGA-BRCA (Breast Cancer): Our largest dataset, comprising 1,231 samples and 5 distinct molecular subtypes.
    \item TCGA-COAD (Colon Cancer): A medium-sized cohort of 524 samples categorized into 4 subtypes.
    \item TCGA-PRAD (Prostate Cancer): A cohort of 554 samples with 8 subtypes, characterized by severe class imbalance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Curse of Dimensionality in Genomics}

From a machine learning perspective, genomic datasets present a classic "high-dimensional, low-sample size" (HDLSS) problem. For each patient sample, we measure $p = 60,660$ gene expression levels (features), while the number of available samples $n$ is several orders of magnitude smaller ($n \approx 500$ to $1,200$). This extreme feature-to-sample ratio creates a high risk of overfitting, where models may capture noise or patient-specific artifacts rather than generalizable biological signals. 

Existing approaches often struggle to balance computational efficiency with predictive power. While deep learning has seen success in other domains, its application here is limited by the lack of massive labeled datasets. Consequently, feature selection remains an essential preprocessing step to reduce the search space and improve model interpretability. However, our findings suggest that selection is not merely a compromise for efficiency; rather, it serves as a critical denoising mechanism that can uncover latent biological signals obscured in the full transcriptomic profile.

\subsection{Contributions}

In this paper, we perform a rigorous benchmarking of feature selection strategies to evaluate their impact on cancer subtype classification. Our contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We establish high-dimensional performance benchmarks using a full-feature Logistic Regression baseline with the SAGA and L-BFGS solvers.
    \item We evaluate the trade-offs between three major feature selection categories—Filter (ANOVA, Mutual Information), Wrapper (SelectKBest, Forward Selection), and Embedded (LASSO, Random Forest)—across three distinct cancer types.
    \item We quantify the impact of class imbalance on feature selection, demonstrating that metrics like Macro F1 are essential for capturing the performance degradation caused by minority subtype confusion.
    \item We analyze a reversal of the "Baseline Paradox," providing evidence that aggressive feature reduction can significantly exceed the performance of regularized full-feature models by mitigating the noise inherent in HDLSS genomic data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

The "High-Dimension Low-Sample Size" (HDLSS) nature of genomic data often leads to the "curse of dimensionality," where sparse feature spaces cause catastrophic overfitting \cite{hdlss_curse}. In TCGA datasets \cite{tcga_resource}, identifying a parsimonious gene subset from 60,660 markers is a biological requirement for interpretability and a computational necessity for model stability.

\subsection{Feature Selection Paradigms}

Feature selection is traditionally categorized into three paradigms \cite{genomic_benchmarking}: 

Filter Methods (e.g., ANOVA, Mutual Information \cite{mutual_info}) are computationally efficient and independent of the classifier. While univariate filters may ignore synergistic gene-gene interactions, they often act as robust noise filters. Our work investigates whether this statistical pruning can actually exceed full-genome performance by removing non-informative transcriptomic noise. Wrapper Methods (e.g., Forward Selection, SelectKBest) treat selection as a search problem using a classifier to evaluate subsets. While they capture feature dependencies, they are computationally intensive on 60,000 features. We address this by benchmarking SelectKBest against iterative Forward Selection to quantify the trade-off between search depth and classification gain. Embedded Methods integrate selection into the objective function. LASSO \cite{lasso_foundational} is a standard for simultaneous regularization and selection, implemented here using the L-BFGS and SAGA solvers \cite{saga_solver}. However, LASSO can struggle with co-expressed gene modules. Alternatively, tree-based embedded methods like Random Forests provide non-linear feature importance rankings; our study evaluates if these rankings offer superior stability across diverse cancer cohorts compared to linear shrinkage.

\subsection{Class Imbalance and Positioning}

A critical challenge in TCGA benchmarking is class imbalance; models often maximize accuracy while failing to identify rare subtypes, such as those in the PRAD cohort. We utilize the Macro F1-score to evaluate how selection paradigms impact minority class identification. Unlike literature proposing singular algorithms, this study provides a comprehensive benchmark across all three paradigms against a high-dimensional baseline. We position our work as a comparative analysis of the trade-off between runtime, sparsity, and balanced performance, clarifying whether feature selection serves as a biological benefit or a performance-enhancing necessity in the presence of noise and imbalance.










\section{Methodology}

Our methodology evaluates the trade-offs between computational efficiency and classification performance in high-dimensional genomic environments \cite{hdlss_curse, genomic_benchmarking}. We implemented a multi-stage pipeline consisting of dimensionality reduction followed by a multinomial classification layer. To formulate the problem, we constrained the problem space across different feature selectors. While specific models required distinct optimization strategies, we maintained consistency by using Logistic Regression for classification and $k$-fold cross-validation where applicable.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

The task is defined as a multi-class classification problem. Given a gene expression matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ with approximately $n \approx 600$ samples and $p = 60{,}660$ features, we aim to predict a discrete label $y \in \{1, \dots, K\}$ representing the cancer subtype. Because $p \gg n$, the primary objective is to identify an optimal subset of genes $p' \subset p$ that maximizes the Macro F1-score while minimizing computational runtime and model complexity \cite{hdlss_curse}.

\subsection{Experimental Pipeline}

The experimental workflow follows a standardized sequence to ensure comparability across selection methods:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Preprocessing: Expression matrices were aligned with subtype labels using TCGA identifiers. Samples were normalized using a standard scaler to ensure zero mean and unit variance.
    
    \item Feature Selection: The dataset was processed through one of three frameworks (Filter, Wrapper, or Embedded) to reduce the feature space from $p$ to $p'$.
    
    \item Model Training: A multinomial Logistic Regression classifier was trained on the reduced set using a stratified 70/30 train-validation split.
    
    \item Evaluation: Performance was measured across ten randomized trials to account for variability in data splitting and initialization.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Feature Selection Framework}

We benchmarked three categories of selection methods to evaluate their impact on performance and dimensionality reduction efficiency \cite{genomic_benchmarking}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Filter Methods: We implemented Variance Thresholding ($\tau \in [0.05, 0.75]$), ANOVA F-tests, and Mutual Information (MI). These methods rank genes independently of the classifier based on statistical properties or nonlinear dependence \cite{mutual_info}.
    
    \item Wrapper Methods: We utilized SelectKBest and Sequential Forward Selection (SFS). For SFS, we applied a variance-based pre-filter to reduce the initial search space to the top 2,000 high-variance genes, evaluating small feature fractions (0.005 to 0.05) to maintain computational feasibility.
    
    \item Embedded Methods: We implemented LASSO (L1 regularization) and Random Forest feature importance. These methods integrate selection into the training process via coefficient shrinkage or impurity reduction \cite{lasso_foundational}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Classification Architecture and Solver Selection}

The classification layer primarily utilized Logistic Regression implemented through the scikit-learn framework \cite{scikit-learn}. Solver selection was strategically varied to balance convergence speed with physical memory constraints.

For our high-dimensional baseline and static filter experiments, we employed the L-BFGS and SAGA solvers. L-BFGS was utilized for the $60{,}660$ feature baseline due to its stable convergence in the presence of extensive regularized features. SAGA was selected for filter experiments because of its optimized performance on large-scale multinomial problems and its specific support for L1 penalties used in embedded comparisons \cite{saga_solver}.

For the iterative wrapper experiments (Forward Selection and SelectKBest), we transitioned to the SGDClassifier (Stochastic Gradient Descent). This transition was a computational necessity; the SGD solver utilizes incremental weight updates that significantly reduce memory overhead.

\subsection{Optimization Objective and Evaluation Rationale}

Models were optimized using multinomial Cross-Entropy loss:

\[
L(\theta) =
-\frac{1}{n}
\sum_{i=1}^{n}
\sum_{k=1}^{K}
y_{i,k}\log(p_{i,k})
\]

A central motivation is the ``Baseline Paradox.'' While high-dimensional baselines often establish a strong performance benchmark, they are susceptible to noise from weakly informative genes. Our methodology tests if aggressive feature reduction can act as a regularizer, potentially exceeding the baseline performance—as observed in our ANOVA results—by isolating the most relevant biological signals.

Given the severe class imbalance in cohorts like TCGA-PRAD, we prioritized Macro F1-score as our primary metric. Unlike raw accuracy, Macro F1 treats each subtype class with equal weight, ensuring that performance on rare subtypes is not obscured by majority class success.

\section{Experiments}

This section details the experimental environment, preprocessing protocols, and evaluation metrics used to benchmark the feature selection frameworks. To ensure a fair comparison, all experiments were conducted using a consistent software stack and hardware configuration.

\subsection{Dataset Preparation and Preprocessing}

The raw transcriptomic data from the TCGA cohorts (BRCA, COAD, and PRAD) was processed to ensure consistency across samples. We applied a standard scaling transformation to the feature matrices, centering the mean to zero and scaling to unit variance. This step is critical for the convergence of the L-BFGS, SAGA, and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) solvers utilized in our classification pipeline.

For each cohort, we implemented a 70:30 train-validation split. The training partition was used for both the feature selection search and model parameter optimization, while the remaining 30 percent was held out as a validation set. This stratification ensures that the reported performance metrics reflect the model ability to generalize to unseen genomic profiles.

\subsection{Reproducibility and Stability}

To account for the variance inherent in random data partitioning and model initialization, we conducted ten independent trials for every experiment using different random seeds. The results reported in this study represent the mean performance and standard deviation across these ten iterations. This rigorous seeding approach allows us to evaluate the stability of each feature selection category and ensures that the findings are statistically robust.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Given the significant class imbalance present in genomic datasets—most notably the PRAD cohort—accuracy alone is an insufficient measure of model quality. We evaluated our models using the following metrics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Accuracy: The overall percentage of correctly identified cancer subtypes.
    \item Macro F1-Score: Our primary metric for comparison. By calculating the F1-score for each subtype independently and then averaging them, this metric ensures that the performance on minority classes is weighted equally to majority classes.
    \item AUROC (Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic): We utilized the one-vs-rest AUROC to assess the diagnostic ability of our multiclass classifiers across varying decision thresholds.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baselines and Computational Constraints}

Our primary control is the Full-Feature Baseline, which utilizes the complete set of 60,660 genes without any dimensionality reduction. This model was trained using the L-BFGS solver to establish a reference performance benchmark. All experiments were conducted on machines equipped with 16GB of RAM and multi-core CPUs. 

Due to the high computational complexity of wrapper-based search algorithms on 60,000+ dimensions, Sequential Forward Selection (SFS) was restricted to evaluating small feature fractions (0.005 to 0.05). For the SelectKBest wrapper, we standardized the evaluation at a fixed feature fraction of 0.50 ($30,330$ features) to assess the impact of broad feature pruning on runtime efficiency and predictive stability.

\subsection{Filter Methods}

Filter-based feature selection methods provided a computationally efficient means of reducing the high-dimensionality of the TCGA datasets while maintaining competitive classification performance. By evaluating genomic features independently of the learning algorithm, these methods scaled significantly better than wrapper or embedded approaches. We performed an extensive sweep of Variance Thresholds ($0.05$ to $0.75$) and evaluated statistical univariate selectors, including ANOVA F-tests and Mutual Information (MI), to identify the optimal subset of genes for subtype prediction. All metrics in this section report the mean across 10 random seeds; per-method standard deviations were small (typically $\sigma \leq 0.04$ for F1 and accuracy).

The results indicate that while Variance Thresholding is computationally cheap—averaging under 5 seconds per threshold across all three datasets—it generally requires a much larger feature space ($\sim$20,000–28,000 features) to remain competitive. In contrast, Mutual Information and ANOVA selectors achieved superior accuracy and AUC with significantly fewer features ($2,000$ to $5,000$). After replacing the original SAGA solver with L-BFGS in the downstream Logistic Regression, ANOVA runtimes collapsed to under 3 seconds per dataset; Mutual Information remained the runtime bottleneck at roughly 170–232 seconds per dataset, dominated by the cost of estimating MI scores across 60,660 features. \paragraph{Analysis of Filter Methods}
The analysis of our filter method sweep, repeated across 10 seeds per configuration, reveals a nuanced trade-off between runtime efficiency and class-balanced performance. ANOVA F-tests emerged as the strongest balanced selector for the BRCA dataset, achieving the highest mean Accuracy (0.8374) and Macro F1 (0.7715) while compressing the feature space to roughly 8\% of its original size. On COAD, Mutual Information yielded the highest Accuracy (0.8650), but its Macro F1 (0.6234) was outperformed by Variance Thresholding at the $0.50$ cutoff (0.6742), illustrating that high overall accuracy does not always imply better recovery of minority subtypes.

A consistent trend across all filter methods was the difficulty in classifying the PRAD dataset, where Macro F1 scores remained notably low ($\sim$0.41–0.47) despite Accuracy values above 0.77. The classification reports suggest that while the models are proficient at identifying common subtypes like PRAD.1-ERG, they frequently fail on rare subtypes such as PRAD.3, 4, 6, and 7, which had zero precision and recall across multiple seeds. This confirms that while filter methods can effectively manage the "curse of dimensionality," they do not inherently solve the issue of class imbalance present in genomic datasets. Additionally, the runtime hierarchy is now firmly inverted compared to the original SAGA-based pipeline: ANOVA $<$ Variance Threshold $\ll$ Mutual Information, with MI roughly two orders of magnitude slower than the other two filters yet not consistently delivering the highest balanced performance.

\subsection{Wrapper Methods}

In our testing, the wrapper methods produced mixed results compared to the baseline and the filter-based selectors. Forward Selection was found to be both significantly slower to run and significantly less accurate than the baseline; even partial searches on BRCA at a few hundred features took several hours. SelectKBest, evaluated at a fixed feature fraction of $0.50$ (i.e.\ 30{,}330 retained features) and averaged across 10 random seeds, also produced mixed outcomes: it underperformed the all-features baseline on every dataset, with the largest gap on PRAD. We concluded that SelectKBest is workable for predicting BRCA and to a lesser extent COAD, but not competitive with simple filter methods on PRAD. \paragraph{Analysis of Wrapper Methods}
Because of the size and dimensionality of the dataset, Forward Selection is not feasible without restricting the candidate pool to a very small subset of features. As illustrated in the Feature Count Reduction plot (Figure~\ref{fig:feature_red}), most effective methods must reduce the feature space by orders of magnitude to remain computationally viable. At the extremely small feature counts required for Forward Selection to terminate, the accuracy and the balance between precision and recall are poor.

The SelectKBest wrapper, run with 10 different seeds at fraction $0.50$, gives a clearer picture of stability than the original single-seed report. On BRCA it achieves the highest mean accuracy of the three datasets ($0.8015 \pm 0.008$), but its Macro F1 ($0.6880$) trails ANOVA's $0.7715$ from Table~\ref{tab:filter_metrics} by a wide margin and exhibits noticeably higher variance on COAD ($\sigma_{\mathrm{Acc}} = 0.049$, $\sigma_{F1} = 0.062$). On PRAD, SelectKBest collapses to $0.6690 \pm 0.031$ accuracy and $0.4194 \pm 0.030$ Macro F1, well below the filter and embedded baselines. Together, these numbers indicate that retaining roughly half of the 60{,}660 genes via a univariate score is not sufficient to recover the predictive structure that the full-feature baseline captures, and that the method is particularly fragile under class imbalance.

\paragraph{Analysis of Embedded Methods}
The multi-seed Random Forest results reveal a more nuanced picture than the original single-seed evaluation. On BRCA, Random Forest achieved a mean Macro F1 of $0.7289 \pm 0.022$, effectively matching the baseline's $0.7268$ while compressing the feature space from 60,660 to a mean of 7,739 genes. LASSO on BRCA similarly reached $0.7231 \pm 0.025$, though its selected feature count varied dramatically across seeds (ranging from $\sim$5,000 to $\sim$58,000), indicating instability in the L1 regularization path depending on the train--validation split. On COAD, Random Forest's mean accuracy ($0.8466$) exceeded the baseline ($0.8320$), but its Macro F1 ($0.5801$) fell short of both the baseline ($0.5825$) and the filter methods (ANOVA $0.5974$, MI $0.6234$), again revealing that higher accuracy does not guarantee better minority-class recovery. The PRAD dataset continued to pose the greatest challenge: despite Random Forest achieving $0.8120$ accuracy, its Macro F1 of $0.4055$ confirms that the model is predominantly predicting the majority subtype (PRAD.1-ERG) while failing on rare subtypes such as PRAD.3, 4, 6, and 7, which received zero precision and recall across all seeds. These results reinforce that the primary bottleneck on PRAD is class imbalance rather than feature selection, and that techniques such as cost-sensitive learning or synthetic over-sampling may be necessary to improve minority subtype recovery.






\section{Discussion}

The results of our benchmarking study reveal a significant shift in the understanding of high-dimensional genomic classification: strategic feature selection can act as a powerful regularizer that exceeds the performance of full-transcriptome models. Our primary insight is a reversal of the expected Baseline Paradox. In the BRCA cohort, the baseline Macro F1 of 0.7268 was significantly surpassed by both ANOVA (0.7715) and Random Forest (0.7289). This suggests that for gene expression data, the high-dimensional space ($p=60,660$) contains substantial noise that can obscure biological signals; by pruning the feature space by over 90\%, these methods isolate a more robust molecular signature.

Furthermore, our analysis of filter methods highlights that statistical simplicity can offer superior generalizability compared to more complex iterative searches. ANOVA and Mutual Information consistently provided higher Macro F1 scores than the Forward Selection wrapper, which plateaued near 0.55 in BRCA despite its focus on feature dependencies. We suspect that in HDLSS environments, the search space for wrappers is so vast that iterative methods are prone to trapping in local optima or overfitting to patient-specific artifacts in the training partition.

The most significant takeaway regarding dataset characteristics is the persistent challenge of class imbalance, exemplified by the PRAD cohort. While the baseline Macro F1 was a mere 0.3811, Variance Thresholding (0.4668) and ANOVA (0.4213) provided the only meaningful improvements. However, even with these gains, the gap between raw accuracy (approx. 0.81) and Macro F1 (0.40) remains vast. This confirms that while feature selection can mitigate noise, it cannot fully compensate for severe subtype under-representation. For genomic datasets with rare subtypes, our results suggest that feature selection must be coupled with specialized sampling or loss-weighting strategies to ensure clinical utility across all patient groups.








\section{Limitations}

While this study provides a broad evaluation of feature selection, several constraints must be noted:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Computational Search Space: Due to hardware limits and the extreme dimensionality of the genome, wrapper methods required a variance-based pre-filter to reduce features to 2,000. This restricted the search space, potentially omitting synergistic gene interactions that were discarded during initial pruning. 
    \item Dataset Bias: Our reliance on TCGA cohorts may introduce biases related to specific sequencing protocols and patient demographics. These results may not fully generalize to other clinical environments or emerging sequencing platforms without further validation.
    \item Methodological Scope: We utilized classical machine learning frameworks to maintain interpretability. Alternative architectures, such as deep autoencoders or graph neural networks, might interact differently with these selection paradigms and could potentially exploit high-dimensional noise more effectively.
    \item Class Imbalance and Metric Gap: Despite the improvements seen with Variance Thresholding in the PRAD dataset, our models still exhibited a wide gap between Accuracy and Macro F1. This confirms that feature selection alone cannot substitute for specialized techniques like cost-sensitive learning or synthetic over-sampling when addressing rare cancer subtypes.
    \item Memory and Convergence: High-dimensional runs utilizing the full $60,660$ feature set placed significant stress on available memory resources. Future studies with expanded hardware capacity would allow for more exhaustive hyperparameter sweeps, particularly for solvers like L-BFGS and SAGA on the full genomic profile.
\end{itemize}







\section{Conclusion}

In this work, we benchmarked filter, wrapper, and embedded feature selection techniques against a high-dimensional Logistic Regression baseline for cancer subtype classification. Our findings indicate that feature selection is not merely a tool for reducing computational overhead, but a vital regularizing step that can significantly improve predictive accuracy. In several cohorts, most notably BRCA, filter methods such as ANOVA provided superior Macro F1 scores compared to the full-feature benchmark, suggesting that pruning transcriptomic noise is essential for isolating molecular signatures in the HDLSS regime.

While ANOVA and Random Forest emerged as the most reliable and efficient selectors across the tested cohorts, the PRAD dataset highlighted the inherent limits of dimensionality reduction in the face of severe class imbalance. Despite the gains provided by Variance Thresholding, the persistent gap between raw accuracy and Macro F1 suggests that feature selection must be combined with balanced sampling or weighted loss functions to resolve rare cancer subtypes effectively.

Future work should investigate the use of ensemble feature selection, where multiple selectors are chained to stabilize the resulting gene sets against the variance of individual folds. Additionally, incorporating interpretability tools like SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations) could help move beyond classification to identify the specific biological pathways prioritized by these models. Ultimately, our study suggests that for genomic classification, strategic feature selection serves as a robust noise filter that can uncover cumulative predictive information often obscured in full-genome models.
